What is Head and Neck Lymphoedema?

Lymphoedema is an accumulation of fluid in the tissues that can be brought on by head and neck cancers and their treatments.

The head and neck region contains over 300 lymph nodes, which are part of a system that helps drains fluid from this area. This system can be damaged by surgery or radiation.

It is normal for swelling to be present for around 3 months post treatments, but this should slowly resolve.

If oedema continues after 3 months, there may be damage to the lymphatic drainage system causing a build-up of fluid. This is likely to be lymphoedema and should be discussed with your head and neck health care team.

Causes

Head and neck lymphoedema is typically caused by damage to the lymphatic system due to surgery or radiation therapy for cancer. This damage impairs the system’s ability to drain lymphatic fluid, leading to swelling. Other causes can include infections, injuries, or congenital abnormalities that affect lymphatic drainage.

Symptoms

  • Swelling that continues 3 - 6 months after treatments
  • Pitting oedema
  • Difficulty swallowing, lymphoedema can be external and internal
  • Reduced movement/tightness and heaviness
  • Symptoms that are worse in the morning and improve over the day when you are up and active
  • Infections called cellulitis
  • If you are experiencing any of the above, please discuss them with your head and neck or lymphoedema team.

Guides

Taking Measurements

Accurate measurements are essential for tracking the progression and management of lymphoedema. Use a flexible tape measure to gauge the circumference of the affected areas consistently over time. Document these measurements regularly and share them with your healthcare provider.

Taking Photos

Photographs can help track changes in swelling and symptoms. Use consistent lighting and angles, and take photos at regular intervals, such as weekly or monthly, to monitor the condition effectively.

Exercising

Exercising

There is much evidence to show that simple activity can significantly help prevent cancer recurrence and also other long-term illnesses, such as high blood pressure, heart disease, kidney disease, type 2 diabetes, and stroke. Cancer and its treatments can cause physical changes and dealing with these is often stressful. Being more physically active can help you cope with and recover from some of these changes.

Being active during and after treatment can:

  • Reduce fatigue.
  • Reduce stress and anxiety.
  • Help look after your bones.
  • Help look after your heart.
  • Help reduce your risk of getting a blood clot.
  • Help keep your weight healthy.
  • Relieve pain.

Why Should I Exercise?

Your neck and shoulders can become stiff and tight:

  • Post surgery
  • During your radiation treatment
  • Immediately after your radiation treatment has ended
  • A long time after your radiation has ended

The exercises in this leaflet will help:

  • Improve lymphatic drainage and help reduce swelling.
  • Prevent you from feeling stiffness.
  • Improve any stiffness you may feel in your neck and shoulders from your radiation treatment.
  • Help the wound to heal and become flexible.
  • Reduce the risk of lymphedema swelling caused by a build-up of lymph fluid.

Why Are Stretching Exercises Important?

Radiation treatment can make your muscles and soft tissues stiff and tight. This condition is called radiation-induced fibrosis. It can occur at any point after you have had radiotherapy, sometimes a few years down the line. This can be painful, change your posture, or affect your joints.

Neck Exercises

Following your surgery: These exercises should be performed three times a day. Try to repeat each exercise up to three times but stop if you become dizzy.

During radiation treatment: Do the exercises every day during radiation treatment.

How long do I do these exercises for? You should do these exercises three times a day until 6 weeks after your radiation treatment is done. We advise continuing with these exercises once a day long-term.

Safety Information

  • You may feel a pull in your muscle but you should not feel any pain. If you feel pain, try not to stretch so far.
  • If the exercises are uncomfortable, you can take your pain medication 30 minutes before exercising.
  • If you have had surgery, some of the exercises may not be possible. You may be told by your surgeon to avoid certain movements after your surgery.

Warm-up Exercises – 10 repetitions of each

  • Deep breaths – breathing in through the nose and out through the mouth.
  • Roll your shoulders in a backward circle.
  • Push your chin forwards and then bring it back to give a double chin.
  • Raise your arm to touch behind your head then down and behind your back, repeat with the other arm.

Facial Exercises – do 5 repetitions of each

  • Say your vowels – AEIOU in an exaggerated manner.
  • Smile.
  • Swallow.
  • Poke out your tongue as far as you can.
  • Open your mouth as wide as you can.
  • Frown and screw up your eyes and face.

Tongue, Throat, Airway, and Jaw Exercises

Front of Tongue

Try to move your tongue around in your mouth as far as you are able. If it is possible, try to clean your teeth with your tongue, starting with the lower teeth (front and back), and then the upper teeth (front and back).

Back of Tongue

Stick out your tongue. Gently bite down and hold your tongue between your teeth (front and back). Swallow your saliva keeping your tongue held in position. Repeat 5-10 times as able.

Throat

You will need a small rubber ball or sponge. Sitting upright, place the ball under your chin and hold it in place. Push your chin against the ball for as long as you can, aiming for a maximum of a minute. Repeat twice. In the same position, push your chin against the ball and release quickly. Complete 30 repetitions of this, building up to doing 3 sets of 30 repetitions.

Airway

Practice swallowing with effort. Hold your breath, push your tongue into the roof of your mouth if you can, squeeze your throat muscles tight and swallow. Repeat this up to 10 times. To make this even more effective, push down on a table or chair arms whilst holding your breath, then swallow.

Jaw

Open your mouth as wide as you can to stretch your jaw. Hold this for about 30 seconds, or for as long as you can.

Performing head and neck exercises with demonstrations

Neck Rotations

Lie on your back with one pillow supporting your head. Slowly turn your head to one side until you feel a gentle stretch on the opposite side. Hold for 20 seconds. Repeat on the other side.

To progress, use the opposite hand on the side of the face to provide a gentle stretch.

Neck Side Flexion

Sit or stand to do this exercise. Tilt your head to one side so that you are bringing your ear nearer to the tip of your shoulder. Hold for 20 seconds. Repeat on the opposite side.

To progress, use the opposite hand to gently apply pressure to your head and add a stretch.

Neck Extension/Flexion

Sit upright. Slowly take your head backwards and hold for 2 seconds. Support your head with your hand if you find this more comfortable. Then, slowly take your head forward to bring your chin towards your chest. Hold for 2 seconds.

To progress, place your hands at the back of your head and gently add pressure when tilting your head down.

Shoulder Shrug and Rolls

Sit upright with your arms relaxed. Shrug your shoulders up towards your ears, hold for a count of 2, then slowly return to the starting position.

Arm Lifts

Lie on your back with one pillow supporting your head and put your arms by your sides. Holding your hands, lift your arms above your head keeping the elbows straight. Hold for up to 20 seconds.

To increase stretch, remove the pillow or hold a small weight like a soup can in your hands.

Shoulder Rotation

Lie on your back with one pillow supporting your head. Clasp your hands behind your neck and point your elbows to the ceiling. Let your elbows fall out to the sides so that you feel a stretch in the front of your chest. Hold for 20 seconds.

Shoulder Blade Squeezes

Sit upright with your arms out to the side. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and hold for a count of five, then relax.

Donning and Doffing Compression Garments

Compression garments are a common treatment for lymphoedema. For head and neck oedema, options include collars or bands to apply gentle pressure and assist lymphatic drainage. Your therapist will look at the best options for you and advise when to wear this.

Using Compression Garments

Whilst compression garments are the main treatment for patients with lymphoedema. Head and Neck oedema remains a difficult area to treat with compression. We do use garments such as a collar or a band to apply compression. Your therapist will look at the best options for you and advise when to wear this.

General Advice and Information

Returning to Driving/Work/Activities

When you return to the above will depend on what your job involves. Your Consultant, Cancer Nurse Specialist, or physiotherapist will be pleased to discuss these with you and give you more specific advice.

Maintaining Good Posture

Try to maintain a good posture when sitting/walking to allow your wounds to heal to the correct length - it may help to sit with a rolled-up towel in the small of your back. It is useful to do your exercises in front of a mirror so if you look like you are not level you can try to self-correct.

How Much Physical Activity is Recommended?

In the UK, adults are advised to do at least 2.5 hours of moderate-intensity physical activity a week. This could be 30 minutes of activity on 5 days of the week, which could be broken up into 10 minutes of activity 3 times a day.

Treatment

Treatment for head and neck lymphoedema typically involves a combination of physical therapy, compression garments, and exercises. Manual lymphatic drainage (MLD) is a specialized massage technique that may also be recommended. Consistent management is key to improving outcomes and maintaining quality of life.

Skin and Oral Care

Proper skin care is crucial to prevent infections such as cellulitis:

  • Moisturize the neck, chin, and cheeks daily with a non-perfumed moisturizer. Apply with gentle downward strokes towards your neck and collar bone.
  • Keep your skin hydrated by drinking plenty of fluids.
  • Use non-perfumed products to cleanse the skin. Pat the skin dry; do not rub or cause friction.
  • Use a high-factor sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher) when exposed to sunlight.
  • Avoid shaving with a razor; use an electric shaver to reduce the risk of cuts. Avoid facial waxing, electrolysis, or facial bleach.
  • Treat any skin breaks promptly with antiseptic cream.

Manual and Simple Lymphatic Drainage

Manual and simple lymphatic drainage is a gentle massage where the skin is stretched along the lymphatic pathway. Regular simple Lymphatic Drainage can help move extra fluid from swollen areas damaged by cancer treatments to areas where the lymphatic system is working well. To be effective Simple Lymphatic drainage needs to be carried out daily and may be a long-term requirement to support the Lymphatic system.

Kinesio Tape

Kinesio tape can be used to help lymphatic drainage by lifting the skin in the affected area or as a support/correction strip.

Kinesio can also be used for scar/ fibrosed tissue

Positioning

Lymphoedema in the head and neck often worsens overnight. Raise the head of your bed or use pillows to elevate your head. A wedge pillow may also help with positioning.

Rollers

Jade rollers and wallpaper rollers are a useful tool for lymphatic drainage of the head and neck and can be purchased by patients for usually under £10s. They are used to help move the fluid through the skin from areas that are struggling to drain to areas that are draining and towards working lymphatics

Before Your Operation

Try to stop smoking: Stopping smoking will reduce the risk of complications before and after surgery, improve your skin’s healing ability, and your long-term health. At the back of this leaflet, you will find the contact details for Smoke Free Life, who can offer support and advice.

After Your Treatment

Scar Care Following Surgery

Scars mainly ‘form’ over the first 2-3 months. It is important over this time to encourage them to be flexible enough to allow you to use your upper body as you did before surgery. One way of doing this is to do the regular exercises shown to you further on in this leaflet; another is to gently massage the scar.

How Should I Massage My Scar?

Once your wound has fully healed, has no scabs, and no sign of infection (normally 3-4 weeks), you can start to massage it. Use a moisturiser, such as E45, available from chemists or on prescription. Gently and lightly massage over your scar with small circular movements for about 5 minutes, once or twice a day, ideally after a bath or shower. This will also help to soften/flatten the scar and make it less sensitive.

How Long Should I Massage the Scar?

Continue for a minimum of 3 months after your surgery, ideally for 2 years as scars can change their shape and continue to tighten for several years. The longer you do it, the better the results will be in terms of scar flexibility and how it looks cosmetically. As your scar will be sensitive to sunlight for several years, a high-factor sun cream is recommended.

Skin Care

Following radiotherapy, moisturise the neck, chin, and cheeks daily using a non-perfumed moisturiser to keep your skin soft and supple using gentle downward strokes towards your neck and collar bone. It is important to keep the skin well hydrated; this can be helped by drinking plenty of fluids. Keep the skin cleansed with gentle products that do not dry out the skin. Pat the skin dry, do not rub or cause friction to the skin as the skin may be fragile following your treatments.

Following your surgery, you may experience changes in sensation around your scar (e.g. numbness, tingling, or extra sensitivity to touch). These sensations are common and often subside a few months after your surgery. Gentle massage or tapping around the area may ease the discomfort and help restore normal sensation. However, you may be left with some lasting numbness.
