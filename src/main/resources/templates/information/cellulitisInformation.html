Cellulitis: information for patients living with chronic oedema / lymphoedema

What is cellulitis?

Cellulitis is an infection of the skin or tissues under the skin caused by bacteria (germs). People with oedema/swelling are prone to cellulitis because their lymphatic system does not work normally. Cellulitis may occur after damage to the skin, such as an insect bite, cut, scratch or athlete’s foot. It can sometimes occur without an obvious cause. Early treatment with antibiotics is very important. If an infection is not controlled quickly, a person may require hospital treatment with intravenous antibiotics (antibiotics given through a drip). There is a risk that cellulitis can damage the lymphatic system and make swelling worse. Occasionally, cellulitis infections can be life-threatening.

How will I know if I have cellulitis?

Your normal swelling may be unusually hot, tender and red. The red colour may spread over a short number of hours. Your swelling may be larger than normal. You may feel unwell with ‘flu-like’ symptoms (fever, shivers, muscle aches, headache, nausea). The lymph nodes near the swollen area may be tender (if you have arm swelling your armpit may feel sore; if you have leg swelling your groin may be sore). Some people often have red, tender swelling which may be due to causes other than infection. In these cases it is more difficult to diagnose cellulitis.

What do I do if I think I have cellulitis?

  • Contact your doctor immediately. If it is ‘out of hours’, contact an out of hours medical service. The doctor may find it helpful to see a photograph of what your swelling looks like normally.
  • If the doctor confirms you have cellulitis, you should start treatment with antibiotics as soon as possible.
  • Rest as much as you need to.
  • If you need a painkiller, regular paracetamol is recommended.
  • You may need to remove compression garments from the infected area if they are too tight and uncomfortable. Some patients choose to wear an old, looser garment.
  • If you usually do lymphatic drainage massage, stop until the cellulitis is better.
  • Ideally, you should return to wearing your normal compression garments as soon as possible. You may need a new garment if your limb remains more swollen after the infection gets better.
  • Let your lymphoedema team know each time you’ve had an episode of cellulitis. Tell them if your garment does not fit well after an infection.

How can I protect myself from developing cellulitis?

  • Take good care of your skin.
  • Use a moisturiser every day.
  • Try to avoid cuts, insect bites, and other injuries to the affected part of your body:
    • Avoid walking barefoot if you have swollen feet.
    • Use an electric razor to reduce the risk of cutting your skin while shaving, or don’t shave at all!
    • Take care when cutting your nails.
    • Wear comfortable, well-fitting shoes.
    • Wear gloves while gardening if you have oedema in your arms.
  • If your skin gets broken: clean the area, apply an antiseptic, and monitor. Seek professional advice if the wound is not healing.
  • Treat athlete’s foot as soon as possible.

Is there anything else?

If you have had cellulitis before, your doctor may choose to prescribe you a ‘just in case’ course of antibiotics, so that if you think you are getting cellulitis you can start treatment quickly. This is particularly important if you are travelling to an area where immediate medical help may not be available.

You should always seek medical attention as soon as possible if you have started taking antibiotics for a possible cellulitis.

If you are prone to infections - if you have had several episodes of cellulitis - you may need long-term antibiotics (the doctor calls this ‘antibiotic prophylaxis’). You should contact the lymphoedema clinic who will discuss this further with you.
